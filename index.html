Octomer Clustering
Heat Mapping

Octomeric (8 base pair long) segments of DNA upstream of promoter sites are constructs of interest in eukaryotic genomics as they often contain sequence elements essential for transcription-promoting enzyme binding. These enzymes create RNA which are "translated" into proteins and thus "transcription" is a dogmatic biological process. By generating a database of 48 octomeric sequences (every possible combination of 8 nucleotides with the 4 bases A, T, C, and G) and aligning each octomer to Vitis vinifera genetic sequences upstream of respective transcription start sites, we can quantify and visualize which sequences have the highest alignment frequency. Sequences with greater impact on transcription enzyme recruitment may have a higher clustering factor with similar sequences, as can be seen in the resultant heatmaps' rough groups. By correlating highly clustered sequences with octomeric sequences known to enhance promoter element binding, we can extrapolate these data to other cultivars and species related to V. vinifera

Differential
Gene Expression

Genes are not always constitutively expressed or expessed to the same degree at all times. Expression varies due to environmental stresses and physiological changes. By sampling large and small grape berries from V. vinifera plants and sequencing each samples' total RNA transcript collection, called the transcriptome, we can measure differences in gene expression potentially related to a physical size difference. The four scatter plots to the left track differential gene expression in 4 samplings over the course of 121 days after anthesis, measuring relative (counts per million, logCPM) and absolute (total counts, logFC) expression. Differentially expressed gene points are coloured red; genes with statistically insignificant changes in expression are coloured black. Finding genes involved with berry size differences is of interest because smaller berries have been posited to produce richer wines, as they have a higher skin/flesh ratio; berry skin contains anthocyanins and other pigments, aromatics that give wines their color and some flavors.

Wax pathway
meta-analysis

The wax synthesis (WS) pathway found in many plants produces waxes and cuticle constituents that protect plants from environmental damage and water loss. Because nearly every plant species contains genetic components related to this pathway, it is thought to be highly important for plant fitness. In order to quantify a degree of evolutionary conservation of WS genes, and thereby pathway function, we can determine the rates of synonymous (Ks) and nonsynonymous (Ka) mutations by looking at nucleotide and amino acid substitution rates. Synonymous substitutions denote silent nucleotide sequence mutations, which result in no change to the resulting amino acid (protein) sequence; nonsynonymous substitutions are those that result in amino acid sequence changes. A Ka/Ks ratio above 1 indicates a genes' evolutionary movement away from original WS pathway function, perhaps specialization towards another function. By extracting Ka/Ks ratios for WS pathway genes and homologs across several plant species closely related to and including V. vinifera, we can compare evolutionary conservation amongst WS pathway genes.
